PyHMMER

Hartsough & Tabor

  • Journal: In Preparation
  • Year: 2017

A fast, convenient genome search tool built on HMMER.

PyHMMER was built to enable high-throughput genetic part-mining: searching genomeic databases for proteins containing particular sequences of domains and co-localized on the genome. We realized that while there are many tools to do individual parts of this workflow, or to perform this workflow for very specific classes of proteins, there exists no flexible, programmable and high-throughput method of part-mining in this manner.

Built on the popular HMMER tool, and written in Python, PyHMMER can quickly and easily search for proteins with any desired Pfam domain sequence (or custom-defined domains). It can also hierarchically find co-localized clusters of proteins matching different domain architectures (e.g. all instances Protein A (Domains 1, 2 & 3) within 200bp of Protein B (Domains 3 & 4, and not 5) on the genome). Together, this becomes a powerful tool for finding biologically-relevant protein clusters in genomes, like bacterial two-component sensors, toxin-antitoxin systems, CRISPR islands, and more!

An open hardware platform for optogenetics and photobiology

Gerhardt, Olson, Castillo-Hair, Hartsough, Landry, Ekness, Yokoo, Gomez, Ramakrishnan, Suh, Savage & Tabor

  • Journal: Scientific Reports
  • Year: 2016
  • DOI: 10.1038/srep35363

A fast, convenient genome search tool built on HMMER.

One substantial barrier in the fields of optogenetics and photobiology is the necessity to either build or buy light hardware, which can require substantial expertise, time, and funds to implement. Frustrated by this, our lab set out to build a simple hardware platform suitable for a wide range of common experiments that was also easy for anyone to build and use.

The result is a 24-well plate device we call the Light Plate Apparatus (LPA). It is composed of a circuit board that enables independent control of 2 LEDs in each well and cheap, 3D-printed plastic structural scaffolds. Using commercial fabrication, an LPA device can be made for about $400, but access to a 3D printer and a bit of soldering knowhow drops this price to $150 or less. Similarly, programming the dynamic LED output has been dramatically simplified by an online web interface capable of simply defining complex light courses and simulating the results for quick and easy debugging. Finally, all of the hardware designs and software are available as an open-source repository to allow others to easily modify and extend the LPA platform to suit them.

Our hope is that the entire field can benefit from easy access to such hardware, allowing greater experimenal consistency among established labs as well as newcomers to be enticed to consider the applications of optogenetics in their scientific pursuits. For more information, see the open-access publication or visit the Tabor Lab webpage.

How Synthetic Biology Will Solve Biological Mysteries and Make Humans Safer in Space

Hartsough

  • Blog: PLoS Synthetic Biology
  • Year: 2016

An opinion article explaining the status and future of synthetic biology in space.

One of the challenging aspects of human spaceflight is that many biological systems, including humans and the commensal bacteria living inside us, are physiologically perturbed in microgravity. At the large scale, humans develop issues in many organ systems, including the eyes, musculature, and bones. Worse, at even smaller scales bacteria also experience dramaitc changes in space that can make pathogens more virulent and resistant to antibiotics.

To learn how to address these problems, and perhaps even engineer organisms resistant to microgravity stress, we will have to turn to synthetic biology. It will give engineers the tools to investigate the biological mechanism of this phenomenon and, more excitingly, to develop engineered organisms that can help humans expand into long-duration space missions, aiding with tasks from food production, to pathogen protection and resource mining.

Characterizing bacterial gene circuit dynamics with optically programmed gene expression signals

Olson, Hartsough, Landry, Shroff & Tabor

  • Journal: Nature Methods
  • Year: 2014
  • DOI: 10.1038/nmeth.2884

We demonstrated the ability to program arbitrarily complex gene expression profiles in E. coli using optogenetics.

We set out to characterize the previously-published Tabor Lab optogenetic sensor proteins (Cph8/OmpR & CcaS/R), which convert light inputs to gene expression outputs. Using custom optogenetic hardware, we used a series of step-responses to assess the dynamic response of each of these sensors to light input. This was then used to parameterize a quantitative model that allowed us to predict the output of each sensor for any series of light inputs.

With this predictive power, we realized that we could use such a model in reverse to calculate the exact series of light inputs (green dashed line) required to achieve a specified output, like the arbitrary curve to the left (black line), which we were able to do with over 95% accuracy (green dots).

The ability to remotely program arbitrarily complex gene expression patterns in cells has never before been achieved with such precision. This capability enables the light sensors to act as the biological analogs of electronic function generators, which are used to debug and characterize other downstream circuits (right). We applied this analogy to the CcaS/R light sensor by connecting its output to both a reporter gene (sfGFP) and a simple downstream gene circuit (TetR). We then used light to program various dynamic output signals from the CcaS/R system, through the TetR inverter circuit, and finally to TetR's output with another reporter gene (mCherry).

In this way, we used the CcaS/R light sensor to dynamically characterize the input-output characteristics of the TetR gene circuit, which serves as a proof of principle for more complex circuits. As circuit characterization is a crucial method for electrical engineers to debug and engineer electronic circuits, so too do we believe our method will enable bioengineers to better construct and debug novel biological circuits.

Iris

Hartsough, Landry & Ekness

  • URL: http://iris.taborlab.rice.edu
  • Language: JavaScript+AngularJS
  • GitHub

A simple web-based programming interface for the Tabor Lab optogenetic hardware

The Tabor Lab has developed an open-source hardware platform for standardizing optogentic and photobiology experiments and to help more people apply optogenetics in their own labs. To facilitate programming the LEDs in this device for use in exposing cells to dynamic light patterns, we have also developed a web-based visual programming interface, called Iris.

Iris allows anyone to quickly and easily get their hardware up and running by helping users specify each aspect of their light program in the left panel, and immediately seeing its effect in the simulation panel on the right. Users can check how their light program behaves accross the entire plate (shown) or full time traces for individual LEDs in a well. Once the desired program is complete, the user simply downloads the compiled binary file to an SD card and inserts it into their LPA device to start the program.
